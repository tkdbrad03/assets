Regulatory &
Environmental Framework

Cherokee County Zoning

Operates under "Outdoor Recreation Facility, Commercial" land-use classification via Special Exception Permit through the Cherokee County Planning Commission. The 2023 CBS Surveying boundaries establish safe buffer zones from adjacent owners.

SC DES Stormwater & Land Disturbance

Construction footprint exceeds 1 acre, requiring active NPDES environmental discharge coverage:

  • Engineering C-SWPPP: Licensed SC Professional Engineer compiles a site-specific Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan detailing sediment barriers protecting Jumping Branch and Kings Creek.
  • Digital ePermitting: All specs and hydrological modeling uploaded to SCDES digital portal. Review cycles typically 40–70 calendar days.
  • Pre-Construction Notification: SCDES stormwater personnel notified seven days prior to groundbreaking for mandatory on-site startup inspection.
Clean Water Act Wetlands Compliance

The course architect routes play across natural high-ground zones, completely avoiding permanent wetland or stream modifications over 0.5 acres. This preserves eligibility for Nationwide Permit 39 (Commercial Developments) via the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, avoiding lengthy Individual Permit review cycles.

Design & Agronomic Team

Course Architect &
Superintendent Plan

Course Design Philosophy: Tecora Ranch will be designed by a licensed golf course architect specializing in minimalist, low-budget executive layouts — a growing specialty within the Southeast U.S. market. The architect's mandate is to route all 9 holes entirely within the 25-acre Operations Zone, using the natural elevation changes, existing tree corridors, and the 1.5-acre irrigation pond as strategic design features rather than engineered obstacles.

Architect Selection Criteria
  • Southeast Regional Specialist: Architect must have demonstrable experience designing 9-hole executive or par-3 courses on budgets under $500K in the Carolinas or Georgia.
  • Native Turf Expertise: Must have experience specifying Bermudagrass or Zoysia varieties appropriate for the Cherokee County climate without intensive chemical maintenance programs.
  • Push-Up Green Design: Must be comfortable designing push-up native soil greens — a deliberate cost-reduction and maintenance-reduction strategy central to this project's lean model.
  • Routing Target: Primary candidates include Tom Jackson Golf Design (Charlotte, NC) and local Upstate SC civil engineers with golf routing experience.
Year 1 Grow-In: Agronomic Plan

The grow-in year (Year 1) is the most agronomically critical period of the project. The following plan governs turf establishment before public opening:

  • Agronomist Consultant (Month 1–3): A licensed South Carolina agronomist is retained at project start to conduct a soil analysis of the 25-acre Operations Zone, specify the correct Bermudagrass or Zoysia cultivar for the native soil greens, and design the grow-in fertilization and mowing schedule.
  • Sprigging / Seeding (Month 3–4): Fairways and tee boxes are sprigged with Bermudagrass immediately following rough grading. Greens are seeded with the agronomist-specified variety. Target: 80% turf cover by Month 8.
  • Irrigation Commissioning (Month 4): The 1.5-acre pond pump system is installed and pressure-tested. Automated pop-up heads are staged along fairway corridors and green surrounds.
  • Part-Time Grounds Staff (Month 4–12): One experienced groundskeeper is hired part-time to manage mowing frequency, hand-watering of greens, and erosion monitoring during grow-in.
  • SCDES Inspection Compliance: Monthly photographic documentation of sediment barrier condition along Jumping Branch and Kings Creek is maintained throughout the grow-in period for NPDES compliance.
  • Soft Opening Target: Month 13 (beginning of Year 2). Course is opened for member preview rounds before full public launch.

Commercial Safety Net

SBA 504 Loan
Financing Strategy

⚠ Important Correction: The USDA Business & Industry (B&I) Guaranteed Loan Program explicitly lists "golf courses or golf course infrastructure" as an ineligible use of funds per 7 CFR 4279. The original prospectus referenced this program in error. The correct commercial financing vehicle for this project is the SBA 504 Loan Program, which has been successfully used to finance golf course construction and equipment nationwide.

Why SBA 504 Is the Right Vehicle
  • Golf Courses Are Explicitly Eligible: Unlike USDA B&I, the SBA 504 program finances the purchase, construction, and improvement of golf courses. Multiple courses across the Southeast have closed using this program.
  • Low Down Payment: SBA 504 requires only 10–15% down for a startup special-purpose property — meaning the $355K liquidity pool can cover the equity injection requirement comfortably.
  • Long-Term Fixed Rate: 504 loans carry 20–25 year fixed rates, currently in the 6–7% range, with no balloon payments — predictable and manageable for a growing operation.
  • Up to $5.5M Available: The maximum 504 loan is $5.5M per project — well above the modest finishing capital this course would ever need to draw.
  • Paired with SBA 7(a): A supplemental SBA 7(a) loan (up to $5M) can be layered alongside the 504 to cover working capital, equipment (carts, mowers), and soft costs not eligible under the 504 structure.

The full Operating Agreement will be drafted by a licensed South Carolina business attorney prior to any subscription being accepted. The following outlines the core provisions that will govern member rights, distributions, and management.

Management Structure
  • Managing Member: Tasha McCray serves as sole Managing Member with authority over all day-to-day operational decisions — staffing, pricing, scheduling, vendor contracts, and capital expenditures under $15,000.
  • Member Vote Required: Capital expenditures exceeding $15,000, any debt financing, sale of the facility, admission of new members, or amendment of the Operating Agreement require a majority vote of all Class A members (each 5% stake = 1 vote; Managing Member retains 70% voting block).
  • Managing Member Compensation: Tasha McCray draws a Managing Member salary of $42,000/year beginning in Year 2, structured as a guaranteed payment before profit distributions. This is included in the operating expense model.
Distribution Policy
  • Reserve Requirement: The LLC shall maintain a minimum cash operating reserve equal to 3 months of fixed operating expenses (~$45,000) at all times before any distributions are made.
  • Distribution Timing: Distributions are declared annually, within 90 days of fiscal year-end (December 31), by Managing Member vote with Class A member concurrence.
  • Tax Treatment: Tecora Ranch Golf Club, LLC is taxed as a partnership (pass-through). Each member receives a Schedule K-1 annually reporting their pro-rata share of LLC income, losses, and deductions. Members are responsible for their own federal and state income tax obligations on distributed and undistributed allocated income.
  • Year 1 Distribution: No distributions in Year 1 (grow-in). All capital is preserved for turf establishment and infrastructure completion.
Dissolution & Buyout Provisions
  • Voluntary Dissolution: Requires approval of Managing Member plus members holding at least 60% of total equity interests.
  • Member Buyout: Any member wishing to exit must first offer their interest to the Company at appraised value (independent appraisal required). If the Company declines, the interest may be offered to existing members pro-rata before any outside sale.
  • Death or Incapacity: A deceased or incapacitated member's interest transfers to their designated heir. The heir receives economic rights (distributions) but voting rights are suspended for 12 months pending Operating Agreement review.

Tecora Ranch is designed first and foremost as a generational asset — a self-sustaining recreational business that pays its founders indefinitely. However, the LLC Operating Agreement will define clear liquidity pathways for members who wish to exit or for the enterprise as a whole.

Path 1: Hold & Distribute (Primary Strategy)

The default plan. The LLC operates indefinitely, distributing annual profits to all six Founding Members per their 5% stakes. Path 2: 18-Hole Expansion & Repositioning (Year 5–7)

Path 3: Sale to Regional Operator (Year 7–10)

Golf course management companies and regional operators actively acquire profitable daily-fee and semi-private courses in the Southeast. A stabilized course generating $293,000+ in annual net operating income would trade at a capitalization rate of approximately 8–10% — implying a sale price in the range of $2.9M to $3.7M at Year 5 NOI. Each Founding Partner's 5% stake would yield $145,000 to $185,000 in a sale scenario — a 4–5x return on their $35,000 investment, excluding years of prior distributions.

Path 4: Conservation Easement Additional Value

The 34-acre Conservation Zone, permanently protected under the easement, may qualify for additional conservation tax credit monetization as federal and state conservation incentive programs evolve. This is a long-term optionality play that carries no downside — the easement is already placed regardless.

Investor Presentation

Founding Partner
Q&A Cheat Sheet

Direct, facts-based counter-points for handling critical scrutiny from invitees during the Founding Partner showcase.

Q: "Golf courses cost millions to build. How can you build a 9-hole layout for just $355,000 in starting cash?"

We aren't building a traditional corporate course — we're building a minimalist, nature-first executive layout. Our 59.01 acres already has perfect natural terrain. By using a dirt exchange to get our 1.5-acre pond dug for free, selling our own timber to clear fairways, and building low-maintenance push-up greens from native soil, we cut traditional construction costs by over 70%.

Q: "What happens if a major rainstorm washes out the course? Who pays for repairs?"

We've engineered risk out of the layout. We have zero sand bunkers — natural grass depressions can't wash out. Our most flood-prone acreage along Kings Creek is locked inside the permanent Conservation Easement. The playing corridors sit on the high northern plateau. Our USDA B&I line of credit acts as a commercial safety net, never touching the partners' pockets.

Q: "If the Conservation Easement is permanent, does that ruin the resale value or stop future expansion?"

No — it protects our core asset. The easement covers 34 acres of steep ridges and creek buffers that are unbuildable anyway. Our explicitly carved-out 25-acre Build Zone remains 100% free of easement restrictions, giving us full room for 9 holes, a driving range, and clubhouse. The easement hands us $112,500 in upfront cash without surrendering a single square foot of our playing arena.

Q: "How can a 9-hole executive course compete with established 18-hole country clubs?"

We aren't competing with them — we're capturing the market they're losing. Traditional 18-hole courses take 5 hours and cost thousands in mandatory monthly dues. Modern golfers along the I-85 corridor want premium, fast golf that can be played in under 2 hours on the way home from work. We provide elite, accessible golf with zero time waste.
